Introduction

In all aspects of research and development, the creation of Secrets of Craft and Nature in Renaissance France has brought together scholars, students, researchers, practitioners, makers, organizers, advisors, developers, instructors, designers, and supporters.

The Edition presents the culmination of over five years of iterative, interdisciplinary, and collaborative work—of countless dedicated and enthusiastic members, individuals, and networks of the Making and Knowing Project.

The Project, and thus the Edition, have more components than a traditional scholarly edition, making recognition and crediting more complex than conventional practices in the humanities.

An exemplary model of crediting has been formulated in “Off the Tracks: Laying New Lines for Digital Humanities Scholars,” and we strive to adhere to their guidelines as stated in the Collaborators’ Bill of Rights.

For more about the creation of the edition, see About the Edition and Pamela H. Smith, Making the Edition of Ms. Fr. 640.

Text Workshops in Paleography, Translation, and Digital Encoding

Over the five years of text workshops, the student participants helped the Project to define the task and workflow of the collaborative and distributed transcription, translation, and encoding of Ms Fr 640. Guided by Lead Paleographer Marc Smith, Director Pamela Smith, and Digital Lead Terry Catapano, they learned and applied principles of paleography and approaches to reading historical hands, language- and culture-specific conventions of scholarly translation, and the skills for producing well-formed mark-up according to a custom schema. Working between French and English language contexts, participants built a diverse and international network of colleagues while gaining hands-on experience in textual research methods and project design and management.

Director: Pamela H. Smith

Lead Paleographer: Marc Smith

Digital Lead: Terry Catapano

Digital Consultant: Alex Gil

Senior Translation Participants: Heather Wacha and Soersha Dyon

2014 (3 weeks, Columbia University)

Priority was placed on recognizing and reading the hands in Ms. Fr. 640 to produce a transcription and a working translation of the moldmaking and metalworking entries. Participants helped source resources for transcription/translation; communicated needs, problems, and progress; negotiated between large- and small-group collaborative work; helped scope the Project’s workflow for different scales and degrees of finish.

2015 (3 weeks, Columbia University)

2016 (3 weeks, Columbia University)

New workshop participants were trained by returning ones under an apprenticeship method. Transcription and translation performed in pairs at the level of the folio. Preliminary markup schema devised and applied. Full transcription completed to high accuracy in 2016.

2017 (2 weeks, Université Toulouse-Jean Jaurès)

2018 (2 weeks, Université Toulouse-Jean Jaurès)

Workshops open to returning participants only and co-organized by Soersha Dyon and Heather Wacha, who developed the translation and markup protocols. Workflow shifted to small groups translation based on areas of expertise. Participants came to understand the stakes of vocabulary normalization for translation, and they refined and implemented the markup protocol.

2018 (1 week, Toulouse)

2018 (1 week, Columbia University)

Participation by invitation for experienced participants with highly specialized skills and vocabulary to finalize the full translation and encoding of Ms. Fr. 640. In 2018, the workshop was joined by invited experts on weaponry, arms, and armor: Pascal Brioist and Jonathan Tavares.

2019 (6 months, Columbia University)

Project Director, Assistant Director, Project Assistant, Digital Lead, and Postdocs Tianna Helena Uchacz, Tillmann Taape, Clément Godbarge, and Sophie Pitman implemented an extensive series of consistency checks to reconcile discrepancies from five years of iterative work based on evolving translation and markup protocols.

Craft & Science: Making Objects in the Early Modern World Laboratory Seminar Participants

This hybrid laboratory-discussion graduate seminar offered through Columbia’s History Department combined historical research with hands-on skill-building and reconstructions of Ms. Fr. 640’s recipes in the Making and Knowing Laboratory. Student final essays for this course appear in this digital critical edition under Essays.

Each semester of the Craft and Science Lab Seminar was driven by a guiding theme to draw together components of the manuscript and provide focus for analysis and activities, as described below.

Digital Humanities Seminar Participants

2017 Spring: What is a Book in the 21st Century? Working with Historical Texts in a Digital Environment

Instructors: Pamela H. Smith and Terry Catapano with Tianna Helena Uchacz and guest lectures by Steven Feiner

Using the Project’s working English translation of Ms. Fr. 640, participants considered issues in textual criticism and editing, and they prototyped a minimal digital edition of the manuscript text using a collaboratively determined intellectual agenda for semantic markup.

2019 Spring: Transforming Texts:Textual Analysis, Literary Modeling, and Visualization

Instructors: Pamela H. Smith, Dennis Tenen, Tianna Helena Uchacz, Steven Feiner with Terry Catapano and Carmine Elvezio

Using the Project’s working transcriptions and English translation of Ms. Fr. 640, participants considered issues in the materiality of the book and digital text and prototyped explorations of the manuscript drawing on methods in digital mapping, textual analysis, and augmented reality.
